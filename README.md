# Quality-paymentsin-health-sector

🏥 Quality-Payments in the Health Sector: Behavioral Responses
Theoretical Predictions & Empirical Evidence on Hospital Incentives

This repository contains the research and analytical work for the project: "What are the theoretical predictions regarding hospitals' behavioral responses to quality-linked payments?" The study explores how different payment mechanisms—Fee-for-Service (FFS), Prospective Payment Systems (PPS/DRG), and Value-Based Purchasing (VBP)—shape clinical and administrative behavior.
🧐 The Economic Challenge

Traditional payment systems often face a trade-off between cost control and quality. While DRGs maximize efficiency, they risk "skimping" on quality. This project analyzes how Value-Based Purchasing (VBP) and Pay-for-Performance (P4P) models attempt to bridge this gap by linking remuneration to observable quality indicators.
🛠️ Theoretical Framework

The analysis is built upon several foundational economic and health models:

    Donabedian’s Metrology: Categorizing quality into Structure (human/tech capital), Process (scientific protocols), and Outcomes (patient welfare).

    The Multitask Trap: Based on the Holmström & Milgrom (1991) model, explaining how "Teaching to the test" occurs when agents prioritize measured metrics over essential unmeasured tasks.

    Selection Dynamics: Exploring Cream Skimming (selecting low-risk patients) and Administrative Upcoding (exaggerating severity for higher reimbursement).

📂 Repository Contents

    Health_economics_final_report.pdf: The full academic paper detailing the comparison of incentives and economic efficiency.

    analysis/:

        Ceiling Effect Simulation: R code illustrating how process measures (like SCIP-INF-1) lose variance and utility once compliance hits 95%+.

        HRRP Impact Visualization: Time-series plots showing the "Teaching to the test" phenomenon where readmission rates drop while mortality rates rise following policy changes.

    tables/: LaTeX formatted comparisons of payment mechanisms (FFS vs. PPS vs. VBP).

📈 Key Insights

    The Ceiling Effect: Process-based indicators eventually suffer from a "ceiling effect," becoming obsolete as hospitals achieve near-universal compliance.

    Strategic Substitution: Empirical evidence from the HRRP Program shows that hospitals may reallocate effort toward financially sanctioned metrics (readmissions) at the expense of clinical surveillance (mortality).

    Risk Selection: Providers can improve rankings simply by "unticking" complex, socially vulnerable patients from their panel rather than improving overall care quality.

    Efficiency Alignment: While VBP aims to align profit with social welfare, its success depends heavily on precise risk adjustment and the size of financial incentives.
